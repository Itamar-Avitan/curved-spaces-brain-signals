A small idea with a very long shadow

Before Einstein, there was a question

Can geometry describe a space that bends?

For centuries, geometry meant Euclid: flat planes, straight lines, familiar triangles. Then Gauss and Riemann asked something more radical—what if the space itself has shape?

Riemann showed how to measure distance and direction inside a smooth curved space—even when that “space” is abstract and has many dimensions. This became known as Riemannian geometry. Half a century later, it gave Einstein the language he needed to describe gravity.

A touch of general relativity

Matter tells spacetime how to curve.

In general relativity, gravity is not treated as an invisible pulling force. Matter changes the geometry of spacetime, and objects follow the straightest possible paths through that curved geometry. Mathematicians call such a path a geodesic.

g
The metric: a distance rule

It tells us how to measure a tiny step at each location.

The path looks curved to us, but it is locally as straight as the geometry allows.

BCI lens

A classifier also needs a distance rule: it must decide whether a new EEG trial looks more like “left-hand imagery,” “right-hand imagery,” a target response, or noise. Choosing the right geometry changes which trials count as similar.

Where the analogy stops

EEG covariance matrices are not physical spacetime, and the page is not applying Einstein’s field equations to the brain. The shared idea is narrower: first identify the objects in your space, then choose a distance rule that respects their structure.

The reveal

Each EEG trial can become one point in a curved data space.

Instead of comparing thousands of voltage samples one by one, we summarize how every pair of electrodes changes together. This summary is called a covariance matrix. It is mirrored across its diagonal and represents a valid amount of variation in every direction. Matrices with these properties are called symmetric positive-definite (SPD). Their curved collection is the SPD manifold.

What this changes in a BCI

Geometry turns “looks similar” into a precise operation.

The purpose is not mathematical decoration. It gives the decoder a consistent way to summarize, compare, average, and adapt EEG trials.

Understand

What pattern is present?

The covariance view exposes coordinated activity across electrodes, which is often what motor imagery and evoked-response decoders need.

Use

Which learned state is closest?

Average the training trials for each state, then compare a new trial with those average patterns using the curved-space distance.

Adapt

Can knowledge move between sessions?

Aligning the matrix spaces can reduce session or participant differences and support transfer learning, though performance still depends on the data.

Monitor

Does this trial look abnormal?

A trial very far from the normal EEG region can be flagged as a possible artifact or poor-quality segment.

Artifacts, weak preprocessing, and unstable relationship estimates still matter. Geometry gives us a better representation and better operations on it; it does not guarantee that every BCI will improve.

Question 4 · learn one class prototype

How can many EEG trials become one representative pattern?

Repeated trials from the same BCI class are similar but never identical. We need one center that summarizes them using the same distance rule that will later judge a new trial.

Plain idea: the center by curved-space distance Mathematical name: Riemannian mean
Question answered What exactly is averaged, and how does that average become a BCI decision rule?

Each ellipse is already a covariance summary of one trial. The chosen center becomes the stored prototype for that class.

Why the examples use ellipses

Two-channel diagonal covariance matrices can be drawn as simple ellipses, so every calculation remains visible. Real EEG uses larger, full covariance matrices; the same geometric definitions extend to those higher-dimensional objects.

Bridge · from geometry to machine learning

A curved surface looks flat when you stay nearby.

Think of a paper map touching the Earth at one location. Near the touching point, the map is useful. The equivalent construction around a covariance reference lets ordinary linear classifiers read curved-space patterns.

Plain idea: flatten the neighborhood around a reference Mathematical name: tangent space, reached by the log map
Question answered How do curved covariance matrices become inputs for the familiar linear classifiers a BCI can train?

The log map turns each nearby covariance matrix into one vector, so tools like LDA, logistic regression, or an SVM can take over from here. This is the alternative to the minimum-distance route.

Put the pieces together

From an EEG trial to a BCI decision.

We now have a structured summary for every trial, a meaningful class average, and a distance rule. That is enough to build a complete classifier.

Plain idea: choose the nearest learned class center Mathematical name: Minimum Distance to Mean (MDM)
Question answered How do the trial summary, the class center, and the distance rule combine into one prediction?

This is the decision the BCI actually ships: measure a new trial’s distance to every class prototype and return the nearest label.

Choose the tool for the job

Geometry is a family of workflows, not one classifier.

These methods share covariance representations but use them differently. A credible BCI study compares them with a strong task-specific baseline.

Question answered Once EEG trials are covariance matrices, which decoder should you actually reach for?

MDM, tangent-space models, and CSP + LDA trade interpretability, flexibility, and calibration cost differently — match the method to the data you have.

Method What it learns Useful when Watch for
Riemannian MDM One covariance center per class Calibration is limited and interpretability matters A simple center may miss complex class boundaries
Tangent-space model A linear boundary in geometry-aware features Classes need a more flexible separator Reference points and preprocessing must stay inside each training fold
CSP + LDA Spatial filters with class-separating variance Motor imagery has two classes and enough calibration data CSP can overfit with few trials and must be fitted inside validation

What geometry does not solve

A better ruler cannot repair a bad measurement.

01

Artifacts

Eye movements, muscle activity, and poor electrode contact can dominate covariance.

02

Changing distributions

EEG shifts across time, sessions, devices, and participants; alignment may help but must be tested.

03

Weak validation

Randomly mixing nearby trials can leak recording-specific information and inflate performance.

04

Missing neurophysiology

A mathematically valid feature is not automatically relevant to the task or user.

Keep the language straight

Plain idea first. Formal term second.

Geodesic · the path whose length gives Riemannian distance.

Riemannian mean · the matrix minimizing total squared distance to its examples.
Local flat coordinates
Tangent space · vectors that let standard linear models use nearby covariance patterns.
